1981: Xerox Star


Introduced in 1981, the Xerox Star was the first workstation that tried to incorporate some of the technologies that are now standard in modern personal computers such as icons, two button mouse, and ethernet networking. The Xerox Star was one of the first systems to have a scrollbar on the right hand side of the window, and to also have a horizontal scrollbar at the bottom of the window. It also had arrow buttons on the top and bottom of the scrollbar that the user could click to move the page. Unlike modern scrollbars, clicking the down arrow, which was located at the top of the screen, moved you further to the beginning of the document. Clicking anywhere in the elevator region would take you to that point in the document.

The Star was developed at Xerox's Systems Development Department (SDD) in El Segundo, California, which had been established in 1977[citation needed] under the direction of Don Massaro. A section of SDD, SDD North, was located in Palo Alto, California, and included some people borrowed from PARC. SDD's mission was to design the "Office of the future", a new system that would incorporate the best features of the Alto, was easy to use, and could automate many office tasks.

The development team was headed by David Liddle, and would eventually grow to more than 200 developers. A good part of the first year was taken up by meetings and planning, the result of which was an extensive and detailed functional specification, internally termed the "Red Book". This became the bible for all development tasks. It defined the interface and enforced consistency in all modules and tasks. All changes to the functional specification had to be approved by a review team which maintained standards rigorously.

The key philosophy of the user interface was to mimic the office paradigm as much as possible in order to make it intuitive for users. The concept of what you see is what you get (WYSIWYG) was considered paramount. Text would be displayed as black on a white background, just like paper, and the printer would replicate the screen using Interpress, a page description language developed at PARC.
